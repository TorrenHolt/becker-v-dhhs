What this wiki covers

The "Unfounded but with Reasonable Concerns" (URC) determination is a child protective services assessment outcome created by New Hampshire House Bill 400, effective June 14, 2017. It sits between a fully Unfounded closure and a Founded finding — acknowledging probable cause of abuse or neglect without meeting the evidentiary threshold for a formal finding.

This wiki synthesizes the complete available document record: workgroup minutes, internal emails, tracking spreadsheets, five versions of Form 1213, four policy directives, Policy 1215 on Administrative Appeals, the AAU proceedings in In Re Torrence H. Becker (Docket No. 2025-1149), and the merits filings from that case.

Central finding
DCYF spent nine years developing policy around a determination that carries indefinite retention, admissibility as pattern evidence, and mandatory administrative review consequences — while maintaining that the determination created no legally protected interest and therefore could not be appealed. The AAU rejected that position in September 2025.

Five themes that run through the record

1. The implementation gap

HB 400 took effect June 14, 2017. Policy Directive 17-72 was not released until January 3, 2018 — a 203-day window during which field staff applied a statutory determination without finalized policy guidance, training, or data infrastructure. Workgroup minutes document significant variation in how URC was used during this period.

2. The approval compression

When PD 17-72 was finally released, it was under year-end leadership pressure. The standard one-month staff review was explicitly bypassed. The Director reviewed and signed documents on return from leave. Internal emails show a four-day approval window for policies that had been in draft for years.

3. The iterative improvement cycle

Each of the four policy directives responds to a specific identified failure. PD 19-10 added a decision matrix to address inconsistent application. PD 22-10 added voluntary service pathways and SOPs. PD 26-03 standardized all closing communications. This is a healthy policy improvement cycle — but one that took nine years to reach its current form.

4. The due process gap

Form 1213, in all four versions through January 2026, notifies recipients of a URC determination but does not advise them of any right to appeal. The AAU ruled in September 2025 that URC determinations are appealable because they create adverse legal effects. The most recent form revision (PD 26-03, January 2026) still does not include appeal rights language.

5. The Becker case

The case of In Re Torrence H. Becker is the first formal adversarial review of a URC determination's factual basis. It surfaces a separate structural failure: DCYF does not track compliance with RSA 169-C:34(VII), the requirement to seek a court order when a parent refuses home entry. The Right-to-Know response confirming this gap was obtained by the appellant himself.

6. The expanding docket — two new appeals

Following the September 25, 2025 AAU ruling, Becker filed two additional appeals: a nunc pro tunc appeal of a February 2023 URC determination (Assessment #992540, Docket 2026-0310) and an appeal of a November 2025 Seacoast District determination (Assessment #1153786, Docket 2026-0311). Both introduce a new legal theory: that all URC determinations issued using standardized Form 1213 violate RSA 541-A:35 because the form contains no findings of fact and no conclusions of law. The March 25, 2026 preliminary hearing produced two oral rulings — good cause denied in 2026-0310, granted in 2026-0311 — and the presiding officer raised a mootness suggestion in 2026-0311 that Becker's combined post-hearing motion contests on six independent grounds.

7. The Cremmen–St. Cyr structural independence issue

Right-to-Know production in the course of these proceedings revealed that Senior ALJ Heather Cremmen maintained an ongoing practice of reporting AAU productivity and docket prioritization to DHHS Chief Legal Officer Melissa St. Cyr — the respondent's top attorney. St. Cyr provided guidance on what was "important to the Department right now." Child abuse and neglect findings were placed last in the informal priority scheme. A concurrent Hiring Waiver Request confirmed the AAU predicted its own federal timeliness violations before Becker's cases arrived. 624 of 629 pending appeals — a 99.2% violation rate — were over 90 days, acknowledged in the Senior ALJ's own words to the respondent's CLO.

8. The BEAR data — first empirical confirmation of scale

BEAR data request #2025196, produced September 25, 2025 by DCYF's own data team from the Bridges SACWIS system, provides the first verified population-level URC volume data in the record. URC runs at approximately 20–22% of all assessments — roughly one in five families assessed. Annual volumes from 2020–2024 range from 1,654 to 2,196 determinations per year, with a five-year total of 10,210 families. URC significantly exceeds Founded determinations in every year by a factor of 2.5 to 3. The ~2,100 annual estimate used in litigation filings was accurate and conservative in peak years.

9. The Weaver complaint and St. Cyr's response

Becker's February 19, 2026 formal complaint to Commissioner Weaver — documenting four systemic RSA 91-A violations by Legal Unit Coordinator Caitlin Sittig — received no response for 38 days, then was answered not by the Commissioner but by Chief Legal Officer St. Cyr. St. Cyr's March 30 response did not address Category B records (system audit logs), did not engage with redaction alternatives, and characterized the Legal Unit's compliance as institutionally sound without addressing specific documented instances. A March 30–31 exchange produced a partial concession: St. Cyr acknowledged the audit log question had not been addressed and reclassified a request first submitted September 25, 2025 as a "new right to know request" — resetting the statutory clock on a six-month-old question.

10. The Shadow System — three levels, all documented

Becker's integrated white paper (v8) synthesizes the full record into a unified theory: DCYF constructed a system operating simultaneously at three levels — a paper system (Form 1213 issued without findings, conclusions, or appeal rights), an operational system (a training methodology instructing workers to "paint the picture," use a "persuasive tone," and "over-write first then strike what doesn't prove an element"), and an immunity apparatus (the nine-year litigation position that URC was not appealable, combined with SOP 1213.2's internal tracking of the appeal window workers were told didn't exist). All three levels are sourced exclusively to the agency's own produced records.

11. The internal/external split originated in 2019, not 2022 — confirmed

Prior analyses attributed the internal appeal window tracking to SOP 1213.2 (PD 22-10, May 2022). Policy 1213 under PD 19-10 (March 2019), page 6, confirmed from primary source (April 2026): workers were instructed to track "the 30-day appeal request time if pending" as a case extension reason. The internal/external split — workers tracking an appeal window the agency was publicly denying existed — was built into official Director-approved policy at the first revision cycle, not the second. This extends the documented contradiction by three years, to six total. SOP 1213.2 formalized a framework already present since March 2019.

12. The delivery gap — an independent constitutional argument

Form 1214 (Founded) is delivered by certified mail with a tracking number field. SOP 1213.2 internally tracks "green card/receipt of delivery is pending" for URC cases. Policy 1215 anchors the 30-day appeal window to "confirmation of service." Form 1213 has no certified mail tracking field and was sent by first-class mail. The consequence: the delivery confirmation required to start the limitations period was never generated for any URC recipient. The clock the agency was internally tracking could not have legally run against any recipient. Every prior URC recipient's appeal window remains tolled on this independent ground, regardless of the notice-of-rights argument.

13. The bidirectionality and structural inversion

The shadow system's three components are directionally agnostic — they operate with equal force whether the agency is marking a family with probable cause or closing a case over a reporting family's objections. The appeal right, to the extent it now exists, runs to the alleged offender (the person named in Form 1213), not to the non-offending responsible adult with legal custody of the child. The child whose safety is the statutory subject of the entire framework has no procedural standing at any stage. DCYF's mootness argument in Docket 2026-0311 — that a corrected letter readdressing the finding to Sarah Becker would moot Torrence's appeal — is, if accepted, the formal adjudicative statement of the structural inversion: standing follows the letter, not the child's safety interest.

Legislative

House Bill 400

The New Hampshire legislation that created the Unfounded but with Reasonable Concerns determination, effective June 14, 2017.
Effective date
June 14, 2017. DCYF issued its first formal policy 203 days later, on January 3, 2018. Field staff applied the determination without finalized policy guidance during this entire interim period.

What HB 400 created

House Bill 400 made several significant changes to RSA 169-C (NH's Child Protection Act). The provisions most relevant to DCYF assessment practice were:

The new assessment determination

HB 400 codified the "Unfounded but with Reasonable Concerns" determination in RSA 169-C:3, XXIX. This created a middle outcome between a clean Unfounded closure (no evidence to substantiate) and a Founded finding (preponderance of evidence). The URC standard requires probable cause — defined in RSA 169-C:3, XXIII as "facts and circumstances based upon accurate and reliable information, including hearsay, that would justify a reasonable person to believe that a child is abused or neglected."

The notification requirement

RSA 169-C:34, II-a requires DCYF, upon a URC determination, to provide a confidential notice to the caregiver or parent containing: (A) recommendations for ameliorative services or protective treatment, and (B) contact information for community supports. This is the statutory basis for Form 1213.

The administrative review requirement

RSA 169-C:34, II-b requires DCYF to conduct an administrative review of all identified reports when a new referral involves two or more prior Unfounded or URC assessments involving any family or household member. This requirement was incorporated into Policy 1172 (Planning the Assessment) and cross-referenced in Policy 1213.

The entry restriction

RSA 169-C:34, VII establishes that DCYF must seek a court order when a parent or caregiver refuses entry to their home. This provision is central to the Becker case — DCYF never sought a court order after Becker's October 4, 2024 refusal, and DHHS confirmed in July 2025 that the agency does not track compliance with this requirement.

URC — Legal Definition

What "Unfounded but with Reasonable Concerns" means in law and practice, and what legal consequences flow from the determination.

The two-threshold structure

The URC determination occupies a deliberately constructed evidentiary middle ground between two established standards:

StandardDefinitionDetermination
Probable causeFacts and circumstances that would justify a reasonable person to believe a child was abused or neglected (RSA 169-C:3, XXIII)Threshold for URC — must be met
Preponderance of evidenceMore likely than not that abuse/neglect occurred (RSA 169-C:13)Threshold for Founded — not met

A URC determination therefore requires DCYF to conclude: (1) there is probable cause to believe abuse or neglect occurred, AND (2) there is not sufficient evidence to establish that it occurred by a preponderance. The determination is not a finding of abuse or neglect, but it is more than a clean clearance.

Legal consequences of a URC determination

The AAU's September 2025 order in Becker identified three specific adverse legal consequences that flow from a URC determination, drawn directly from the statutory framework:

Consequence 1 — Indefinite retention
"Unfounded but with reasonable concern reports" are retained indefinitely. (RSA 169-C:35-a, III) This is the same retention treatment as Founded reports — the URC record does not age off.
Consequence 2 — Mandatory administrative review trigger
If there are two or more Unfounded reports or URC reports involving a family or household member, DCYF must conduct an administrative review of all identified reports when beginning a new child protection investigation. (RSA 169-C:34, II(b)) A single URC report can be half the trigger for mandatory heightened scrutiny on all future referrals.
Consequence 3 — Admissibility as pattern evidence
Evidence of prior founded or unfounded reports of abuse or neglect shall be admissible in RSA 169-C proceedings to establish a relevant pattern or course of conduct. (RSA 169-C:12) Per RSA 541-A:33, a URC report may also be admissible in AAU appeal proceedings.
Due process significance
A URC determination: (1) is held indefinitely, (2) can accelerate DCYF scrutiny on any future referral, and (3) can be introduced as evidence of a pattern of conduct in future proceedings. Yet from 2017 through at least January 2026, the notice letter sent to families receiving this determination contained no advisement of any right to challenge it. The AAU ruled in September 2025 that these consequences are sufficient to establish a legally protected interest — meaning the determination was always appealable, families just weren't told.

What URC is not

The URC determination does not result in placement of the subject's name on the DCYF Central Registry of founded reports of child abuse and neglect. Central Registry entry is the consequence of Founded determinations only. This distinction was central to DCYF's motion to dismiss in Becker — DCYF argued that because Becker's name was not on the Central Registry, he had no legally protected interest. The AAU rejected this argument based on the three consequences identified above.

Field conceptual confusion

The November 2017 workgroup minutes WG-1 document genuine practitioner uncertainty about what URC means and when to use it. Some staff wanted it called "substantiated." Some disliked the "Founded Problem Resolved" category and preferred a court-involved/not-involved binary. The determination's conceptual ambiguity contributed to the inconsistent application that PD 19-10's Assessment Decision Guidelines Matrix was designed to address.

Becker Appeals

Legal Arguments

The statutory and constitutional arguments in Becker's proposed findings and objections. The AAU can reverse the URC determination but cannot adjudicate Fourth Amendment claims directly — those belong to superior court. For the full evidentiary analysis, see Merits Filings.

The core statutory argument — RSA 169-C:34(VII)

The statute is unambiguous: when a parent or caregiver refuses entry to their home, DCYF must seek a court order to enter. Becker refused warrantless entry on October 4, 2024. DCYF never sought a court order — across a 45-day assessment period. Becker's conclusion, articulated in the closing of his objections:

"The Division does not explain why they never sought a court order. They cannot explain it. To seek a court order would have required them to present facts under oath, subject to scrutiny and cross-examination. It would have required them to demonstrate genuine emergency circumstances justifying immediate intervention. It would have subjected their investigation to judicial oversight from the beginning, rather than after the damage was done."Becker OBJ-A — Conclusion

The Right-to-Know confirmation

On July 24, 2025, Becker received a response to his Right-to-Know request from DHHS Deputy Chief Counsel John B. Martin: "The information you are seeking is not tracked by the Department." The information sought was RSA 169-C:34(VII) compliance data — whether DCYF was seeking court orders when entry was refused. The agency that does not track compliance with a statutory mandate cannot demonstrate that it complies. Becker framed this as systemic evidence, not just an individual case failure.

The code enforcement coordination argument

Becker argues that McKenney's October 7 contact with Code Enforcement — within three days of his warrantless entry refusal — constitutes a Fourth Amendment end-run. The constitutional doctrine he implicitly invokes is established in criminal law: using a government agency as an instrument to accomplish what a direct search would require a warrant for is prohibited. Its application in the civil child protective services context is less developed in NH case law. Becker characterizes it as "circumventing constitutional protections" and argues that evidence obtained through Code Enforcement after a refused direct entry cannot form the basis of an administrative finding.

The manufactured crisis argument

Becker argues that DCYF's enforcement of an unsigned safety plan, directing children to a placement that Becker immediately identified as unsafe, and then approving a camping arrangement in 40°F weather for a sick child, constitutes manufactured harm. His proposed findings document that the most dangerous situations the children faced during the assessment period — the transphobic grandparent placement, the camping in freezing weather — were created by DCYF's demands, not by conditions in his apartment.

The coerced consent argument

The October 13 entry — the one that produced Durst's "not uninhabitable or dangerous" observation — was conditioned on Becker's agreement and occurred at 12:15 AM under circumstances Becker characterizes as duress: DCYF threatened to keep a sick child camping in cold weather unless he consented to a midnight police entry. Becker explicitly told Supervisor Vermette his consent was not voluntary.

What the AAU can and cannot do

The AAU's jurisdiction is to review DCYF's decision — the URC determination itself. It can reverse the finding if probable cause was not established. It cannot: order DCYF to provide written acknowledgment that no finding exists (Becker's Relief B — denied); refer the matter to oversight bodies (Becker's Relief C — denied); or issue a declaratory ruling on Fourth Amendment compliance. Becker appears to understand this — his primary relief request (Relief A) is simply reclassification to "Unfounded."

The probable cause question
If evidence obtained after the October 4 refusal — the Code Enforcement findings, the October 7 photographs, the October 29 interview — is excluded or significantly discounted on the grounds it was obtained through constitutionally problematic means, what remains? The pre-October 4 basis was: a City Welfare referral for utility disruptions and housing conditions. Becker had disclosed the plumbing issues to McKenney and explained they were being repaired. The question the AAU must answer is whether that foundation, standing alone, establishes probable cause.

DCYF's legal position

DCYF's motion argued a single, clean legal proposition: the only DCYF action that creates a legally protected interest and triggers AAU jurisdiction is placement of a name on the Central Registry. Since Becker's name was not on the Central Registry, he had no legally protected interest, the AAU had no jurisdiction, and the appeal must be dismissed.

The motion conceded that Central Registry placement "can affect employment or a person's ability to become a legal guardian" and acknowledged that citizens have the right to seek administrative review when DCYF seeks to enter their name on the Registry. But it argued this right was limited to Registry entries only — all other DCYF decisions were not appealable to the AAU.

The AAU's legal framework

Presiding Officer Towle began with the general jurisdiction provisions of RSA 126-A:5, VIII and He-C 201.02(b), which broadly empower the AAU to review "any decision made by the Department which adversely affects an individual" — absent specific contrary statutory language. DCYF offered no specific statutory language exempting URC determinations from review. The burden was therefore on DCYF to demonstrate either (1) URC determinations are specifically exempted, or (2) they do not adversely affect the appellant. DCYF established neither.

The adverse effects analysis

The order identified three specific statutory consequences of a URC determination:

EffectStatutory basis
URC reports retained indefinitelyRSA 169-C:35-a, III
Two or more URC/Unfounded reports trigger mandatory administrative review of all reports on future referralsRSA 169-C:34, II(b)
Prior URC reports admissible in RSA 169-C proceedings to establish pattern or course of conductRSA 169-C:12; RSA 541-A:33

The AAU's conclusion: "As an 'unfounded but with reasonable concern report' is retained by DCYF indefinitely and may be admitted into evidence against an individual to prove a pattern or course of conduct, it is clear such a report has an adverse effect on said individual."

The due process rationale

The order added a due process dimension that goes beyond the immediate case:

"There exists the possibility an 'unfounded but with reasonable concern report' may be entered against an individual without sufficient evidence to meet the probable cause standard, thereby subjecting said individual to an inaccurate, indefinite report which may be used against them. If an 'unfounded but with reasonable concern report' cannot be appealed, then individuals have no method to challenge the factual conclusions drawn by DCYF."AAU Order on Motion to Dismiss, Exhibit W

This paragraph is the most consequential in the ruling. The AAU is stating that DCYF's position — if accepted — would create a class of government-held determinations that carry real, indefinite legal consequences but are entirely insulated from review. That is a constitutionally untenable position, and the AAU declined to adopt it.

What Reliefs B and C reveal

DCYF's motion was granted as to Becker's requests for a written acknowledgment (Relief B) and an oversight referral (Relief C). These are conceded as beyond AAU authority. But the fact that Becker requested them — and the AAU's care in explaining why they are beyond its authority — signals that Becker is aware the AAU is a limited forum and has considered, but not yet pursued, superior court action for the constitutional claims.

Systemic significance

This ruling is the first formal adjudication of the question DCYF left unresolved in December 2017 when it noted Policy 1215 "needs a Bridges process first." The answer is that URC determinations have always been appealable — families just weren't told. The implications for the form, for past URC determinations, and for the Division's systemic practices are substantial and still unfolding.

How the gap developed

The due process gap was visible in the document record from the beginning. The December 2017 tracking spreadsheet TK-1 noted that Policy 1215 (Administrative Appeals) needed attention under HB 400, with the notation "Needs Bridges process first to identify procedures." This was the only note in the tracking document acknowledging the appeal pathway question — and it was deferred indefinitely.

The gap persisted through all four policy directives and all three versions of Form 1213. No one in the policy development chain raised the question of whether URC recipients should be advised of appeal rights. The workgroup minutes, the internal emails, the draft review cycles — none of them contain any discussion of this issue.

The asymmetry with Founded determinations

The contrast with Founded determinations makes the gap concrete. Form 1214 (Notice of Finding, for Founded determinations) contains:

  • Explicit statement: "You have a right to appeal DCYF's decision at an administrative hearing if you dispute the finding"
  • 30-day deadline from confirmation of service
  • Warning: failure to request hearing within 30 days waives the right to administrative appeal
  • A detachable Request for Administrative Hearing form

Form 1213 (Notice of URC) contains: the CPSW's telephone number. That is the complete due process advisement provided to a person who has just received a determination that will be held indefinitely, may be introduced as pattern evidence in future proceedings, and can accelerate DCYF scrutiny on any future contact with their family.

Still open as of January 2026
PD 26-03 was issued January 20, 2026 — four months after the September 25, 2025 AAU ruling. The revised Form 1213 released under PD 26-03 does not include appeal rights language. Form 1214, revised in the same package, retains its full due process advisement. The asymmetry persists.

What remedy looks like

The minimum remediation would be adding to Form 1213 language parallel to Form 1214: a statement that the recipient has the right to appeal the determination to the AAU, the timeframe for doing so, and a mechanism to request a hearing. Whether the 30-day window applicable to Founded determinations applies to URC appeals — or a different window applies — is a question that may need to be resolved through the Becker merits proceeding or through legislative/rulemaking action.

Retroactive implications

URC determinations have been issued since the September 2017 pilot period. The AAU ruled they have always been appealable. Whether families who received URC determinations between 2017 and the date of any remediation, without appeal rights advisement, have any recourse is an open legal question — one that may turn on the applicable statute of limitations for AAU appeals and whether good cause exists for late filings.

The delivery gap — an independent constitutional argument

Beyond the notice-of-rights failure, an independent due process argument exists in the method of delivery. Form 1214 (Founded) is delivered by certified mail with a tracking number field on the form. SOP 1213.2 internally tracks "green card/receipt of delivery is pending" for URC cases. Policy 1215 anchors the 30-day appeal window to "confirmation of service." Form 1213 was sent by first-class mail, generating no delivery confirmation for any recipient.

The legal consequence: because DCYF cannot establish when — or whether — any URC notice was received, the limitations period it was internally tracking could not have legally run against any URC recipient. This argument operates independently of and cumulatively with the notice-of-rights argument. A family that received Form 1213 did not miss a deadline. They were never told a clock existed — and if they had been told, DCYF could not prove when the clock started. The white paper argues that prior URC recipients should receive notification of appeal rights with a tolled window of not less than 90 days from notification, grounded in this delivery gap as well as the notice omission.

Technical resource note
Form 1213 was distributed as an editable Microsoft Word template (.dotx) attached to each policy directive statewide distribution email. Adding the appeal rights language and the certified mail tracking field that Form 1214 has always contained required no additional infrastructure, no new budget, and no process that was not already underway in each revision cycle. The nine-year omission was not a resource constraint. It was a choice made within a revision process that was already running.

What RSA 169-C:34(VII) requires

When a parent, guardian, or caregiver refuses DCYF entry to their home during an assessment, DCYF must seek a court order authorizing entry. This is a statutory Fourth Amendment protection — it interposes judicial review between DCYF's request and the forced entry. It is not discretionary. When entry is refused, the path to entry runs through a court.

The training curriculum connection

Martin's training curriculum production (RTK-3) came in the same production cycle as his RSA 169-C:34(VII) tracking confirmation (RTK-2). The curriculum explains why no tracking exists: the framework governing entry refusal presents Motivational Interviewing clinical management as the operative response to constitutional refusal — not the court order RSA 169-C:34(VII) mandates. Workers are trained to "roll with resistance." The court order does not appear in the training framework as the required statutory response. The admission and its explanation came from the same source, without resistance, in the same RTK production cycle. Two independent DHHS legal offices have confirmed the tracking gap, and the training curriculum produced by the same office explains why the gap exists institutionally.

What DCYF's non-tracking means

"An agency that does not track compliance with a statutory mandate cannot be ensuring compliance with that mandate." In the Becker case: Becker refused entry on October 4. DCYF never sought a court order. Instead: contacted Code Enforcement (Oct 7); obtained photographs through Sarah Becker's unauthorized entry (Oct 7); and coerced consent through manufactured crisis conditions (Oct 13). Whether this pattern repeats in other cases is unknown — because the data does not exist, confirmed twice by two separate DHHS legal offices across two separate RTK requests.

The broader systemic question

The URC framework was designed in part to give DCYF a middle ground between closing cases cleanly and pursuing formal founded determinations. The Becker case raises the question of whether that middle ground is being used as a substitute for the legally required path when parents assert their Fourth Amendment rights. If DCYF cannot enter a home without a court order, but also cannot easily obtain a court order on the evidence available, a URC determination at closure may be more tractable than statutory compliance would require. This cannot be confirmed or denied from the available record — but the tracking gap means it also cannot be disproven.

The three-level shadow system — institutional architecture

The white paper synthesizes the governance record into a unified institutional theory: the failures documented here are not independent errors but three interdependent levels of a functioning system. The paper system (Form 1213 with no findings, conclusions, or appeal rights) makes determinations unreviewable on their face. The operational system (training methodology building persuasive narratives toward predetermined conclusions) generates those determinations through a process insulated from external examination. The immunity apparatus (nine-year litigation position that URC was not appealable, running parallel to the internal 30-day tracking workflow) sealed the system from any corrective review. Eve Allen appears in every document in the record as the central policy figure — from the July 2016 workgroup through the May 2022 policy release. The December 2017 approval process established a pattern of bypassing standard review procedures under time pressure. The one-month staff review was skipped for PD 17-72. Legislative

RSA 169-C Provisions

The specific provisions of RSA 169-C (NH Child Protection Act) most relevant to the URC determination and DCYF assessment practice.

Key provisions

RSA 169-C:3, XXIII — Probable Cause
"Facts and circumstances based upon accurate and reliable information, including hearsay, that would justify a reasonable person to believe that a child subject to a report under this chapter is abused or neglected." This is the threshold standard for URC.
RSA 169-C:3, XXIX — URC Definition
"A report made pursuant to this chapter for which the department determines that there is probable cause to believe the child was abused or neglected, but for which there is insufficient evidence to establish by a preponderance of the evidence that the child was abused or neglected."
RSA 169-C:12 — Admissibility of Prior Reports
"Evidence of prior founded or unfounded reports of abuse or neglect shall be admissible in proceedings under RSA 169-C in order to establish a relevant pattern or course of conduct." Combined with RSA 541-A:33, URC reports may also be admissible in AAU proceedings.
RSA 169-C:34, II-a — Notification Requirement
Upon a URC determination, DCYF must provide a confidential notice to the caregiver or parent containing: (A) recommendations for ameliorative services or protective treatment; (B) contact information for community supports. Statutory basis for Form 1213.
RSA 169-C:34, II-b — Administrative Review Trigger
When a new referral involves two or more prior Unfounded or URC assessments involving any family or household member, DCYF must conduct an administrative review of all identified reports. A single URC report can be half the trigger threshold.
RSA 169-C:34, VII — Court Order Requirement
When a parent or caregiver refuses DCYF entry to their home, DCYF must seek a court order authorizing entry. Central to the Becker case — DCYF never sought a court order after Becker's October 4, 2024 refusal, and the agency confirmed it does not track compliance with this requirement.
RSA 169-C:35 — Central Registry
Founded reports of child abuse and neglect are entered on the Central Registry. URC determinations do not result in Central Registry entry — a distinction central to DCYF's (rejected) motion to dismiss in Becker.
RSA 169-C:35-a, III — URC Retention
URC reports are retained indefinitely. This was one of three adverse effects identified by the AAU in ruling that URC determinations create a legally protected interest.

What Form 1214 contains

Form 1214 (Notice of Finding, PD 26-03, January 2026) is a three-page document sent to persons found responsible for child abuse or neglect upon a Founded determination. It is sent via Certified Mail. It contains:

A person found responsible for child abuse receives a three-page document with a detachable hearing request form and a 30-day appeal deadline. New Forms — PD 26-03 (2026)

The two new forms created by PD 26-03: Form 1220 (Closing Letter to Professional Reporter) and Form 1221 (Unfounded Closing Letter to Parent/Legal Guardian).

Form 1220 — Closing Letter to Professional Reporter

Prior to PD 26-03, professional reporters (mandated reporters such as physicians, teachers, and law enforcement) who made referrals had no standardized mechanism to learn the outcome of their report. Form 1220 addresses this gap, standardizing the closing communication to professional reporters across all District Offices. The form was developed with the Intake/Assessment Workgroup.

Form 1221 — Unfounded Closing Letter to Parent/Legal Guardian

This is the most analytically significant new form. Prior to PD 26-03, an Unfounded assessment closure produced no standardized written communication to the family. PD 22-10's Best Practices section noted that a CPSW "may send a notice" — but this was discretionary. Form 1221 standardizes this practice statewide.

The form's language is notable: "This determination does not mean that the allegations did not happen or that there are not concerns, but there is not sufficient evidence to substantiate a finding." This is careful drafting — an Unfounded closure is not an exoneration, and the form says so explicitly. It also includes a recommendations section, effectively narrowing the gap between an Unfounded closure and a URC closure from the family communication standpoint.

Form 1221 is addressed to "Parent/Legal Guardian" — the same framing as Form 1213. Neither form contains appeal rights language. For an Unfounded determination, the lack of appeal rights advisement may be less legally significant, since an Unfounded determination does not carry the same adverse statutory consequences as a URC determination. However, the RSA 169-C:12 admissibility provision applies to "unfounded reports" as well — prior Unfounded reports can also be admitted as pattern evidence.

The motion failed because it overstated the narrowness of AAU jurisdiction. DCYF needed to show either that URC determinations are specifically exempted, or that they are not adverse. It showed neither. The three statutory consequences the AAU identified (indefinite retention, pattern evidence admissibility, mandatory review trigger) made the adverse effects showing straightforward.

DCYF's formal framework for administrative appeals of Founded determinations. Policy 1215 has been produced and publicly confirmed: it has not been updated since 2017. Confirmed — DHHS website, April 2026
Policy 1215 has not been updated since 2017. This is publicly verifiable from the DHHS website. The December 2017 tracking spreadsheet identified Policy 1215 as requiring revision to address URC determinations, with the notation "Needs Bridges process first to identify procedures." What Policy 1215 governs

Policy 1215 establishes the administrative appeal process for Founded determinations. Section I covers court order findings, appealable to the Superior Court under RSA 169-C:28. Section II covers administrative determinations — appealable to the AAU under RSA 541 within 30 days of confirmation of service of the Notice of Finding. The policy governs the entire process from appeal request through hearing, AAU order, and Central Registry entry timing.

What the confirmed text establishes

Policy 1215's appeal framework covers two categories of determination. Section II lists Forms 1214, 1215, and 1216 as the triggering documents for administrative determination appeals. Form 1213 does not appear in Section I, Section II, Section III, Practice Guidance, or the definitions. URC is not mentioned anywhere in the policy. The gap is confirmed from the document itself — not inferred from its absence elsewhere in the record.

The policy's own due process definition

Policy 1215 defines due process as requiring that "no prejudicial or unequal treatment will result." That is the standard DCYF wrote for itself and applied to Founded recipients — while denying it to URC recipients for nine years. The agency's own policy definition of due process is the standard that Form 1213's absence of findings, conclusions, and appeal rights fails to meet. It is now confirmed that this standard has remained in the policy unchanged since 2017, alongside the unchanged absence of URC from the policy's coverage.

The certified mail anchor

Policy 1215 anchors the 30-day appeal window to "confirmation of service of the Notice of Finding" — language that presupposes certified mail delivery confirmation. Form 1213 was sent by first-class mail; no delivery confirmation exists for any URC recipient. The clock Policy 1215 specifies could not have started running for any prior URC recipient. The policy also addresses Founded recipients who "did not claim their letter" — a fallback mechanism that does not exist for URC recipients, because Form 1213 is not in Policy 1215.

What the non-revision means legally

The AAU ruled in September 2025 that URC determinations are appealable final decisions. That ruling did not require DCYF to update Policy 1215 — the AAU held that existing general jurisdiction provisions were sufficient to authorize URC appeals. But the confirmed non-revision of Policy 1215 means that as of April 2026, no DCYF policy document advises field workers of the URC appeal pathway, no DCYF policy document describes the procedure for URC appeals, and no DCYF policy document provides field-level guidance on what happens when a URC recipient requests a hearing. Workers applying Form 1213 today have no policy framework telling them what comes next if a family exercises the right the AAU confirmed seven months ago.

What the Supplemental RTK will now confirm
The March 6, 2026 supplemental RTK for Policy 1215 requested all versions from January 1, 2017 to the present; records reflecting any decision to revise, defer, or not revise; and records reflecting whether the Bridges prerequisite was ever completed. The DHHS website confirmation answers the threshold question — the policy has not been revised. The supplemental RTK's remaining analytical value is establishing whether any internal records document a decision to defer or abandon the revision, and whether the Bridges prerequisite was ever formally assessed. If no such records exist, the gap was not a decision — it was a permanent deferral that was simply never revisited.

The December 2017 gap — identified, documented, never closed

The December 2017 legislative tracking spreadsheet TK-1 flagged Policy 1215 as needing attention under HB 400 with the notation "Needs Bridges process first to identify procedures." RSA 541-A:35 — Systemic Failure

The Administrative Procedure Act compliance argument introduced in Dockets 2026-0310 and 2026-0311. If sustained, it reaches every URC determination issued since December 2017 — an estimated 17,000–19,400 families.
Active deficiency — systemic scope
RSA 541-A:35 requires that final adverse agency decisions include findings of fact and conclusions of law, separately stated. The AAU ruled in September 2025 that URC determinations are final adverse decisions. Form 1213 — used for all URC determinations — contains no case-specific findings of fact and no conclusions of law. Every URC determination issued using the standard form is facially non-compliant with the APA.

The statutory argument

RSA 541-A:35 provides: "A final decision or order adverse to a party in a contested case shall be in writing… A final decision shall include findings of fact and conclusions of law, separately stated. Findings of fact, if set forth in statutory language, shall be accompanied by a concise and explicit statement of the underlying facts supporting the findings."

The logical chain is sequential and each step is now established: (1) RSA 541-A:35 applies to all final adverse agency decisions in contested cases. (2) The AAU ruled September 25, 2025 that URC determinations are final adverse decisions. Therefore (3) URC determinations must satisfy RSA 541-A:35. (4) Form 1213 contains no case-specific findings of fact and no conclusions of law — only a generic conclusory statement and generic parenting advice. Therefore (5) every URC determination issued using Form 1213 is facially non-compliant with RSA 541-A:35.

Why this is different from the 2025-1149 merits question

Docket 2025-1149 asks whether DCYF had probable cause in one specific case — a fact-intensive merits question. The RSA 541-A:35 argument is a facial challenge to the form of every URC determination regardless of its underlying facts. Even a URC determination fully supported by evidence fails RSA 541-A:35 if issued using the standard form, because the standard form contains no case-specific findings. This is a legal question, not a factual one — and it can potentially be resolved on motion without a full evidentiary hearing.

Internal identification — six weeks before release

RTK-produced December 2017 Workgroup minutes establish that field supervisors identified in the November 5, 2017 Workgroup minutes that the URC letter "doesn't meet statutory requirements" — six weeks before Form 1213 was formally released. That notation appears again in the December 4, 2017 minutes. The RSA 541-A:35 non-compliance was not discovered by litigation. It was identified internally before the form was released and survived four revision cycles unchanged across nine years.

Scale of the deficiency

DCYF issues approximately 2,100 URC determinations annually. From December 2017 through 2026 — eight years — an estimated 17,000–19,400 families received these determinations. Each received a standardized letter with no findings of fact, no conclusions of law, and no notice of appeal rights. Under the RSA 541-A:35 argument, each of those determinations is facially invalid — the administrative equivalent of issuing a court judgment with no findings of fact or legal analysis attached.

Remedy — why expungement rather than remand

Becker argues remand is unavailable for determinations that are years old: contemporaneous evidence cannot be reconstructed; witnesses' memories have faded; due process requires findings to be made at the time of the determination. Reconstructing findings years after the fact, denying the family the opportunity to contest contemporaneous evidence, does not satisfy due process. For older determinations especially, expungement is the only constitutionally adequate remedy.

Public policy weight

The petition notes the institutional cause of the problem: DCYF issued legally deficient determinations for years, maintained they were not appealable, failed to provide required notice to thousands of families, and litigated against the AAU's jurisdiction rather than ensuring compliance with basic procedural requirements. Having received an adverse ruling on jurisdiction, DCYF cannot now assert that families' exercise of confirmed appeal rights creates unfair surprise. The appropriate response to systemic non-compliance with statutory requirements is correction — not continuation.

Becker's formal complaint to Commissioner Weaver (February 19, 2026) alleging systemic RSA 91-A non-compliance by Legal Unit Coordinator Caitlin Sittig — and the March 30–31, 2026 response exchange that produced a partial but significant concession.
Threshold procedural fact
The complaint was addressed to Commissioner Weaver requesting an independent review of the Legal Unit. The response came from Chief Legal Officer Melissa St. Cyr — head of the unit whose conduct was under complaint — who confirmed: "The commissioner is aware of your request and asked me to respond." The Legal Unit conducted its own compliance review.

The complaint — four documented violations

Filed February 19, 2026, addressed to Commissioner Weaver, CC'd Deputy Chief Counsel Martin and AAG Bentley, with seven documentary exhibits. Four systemic violations documented:

  • Failure to respond within 5 business days (RSA 91-A:4, IV) — September 25 SNAP request ignored 29 days; October 28 clarification letter never answered at all
  • Overbroad exemptions — audit logs and inter-agency communications treated as confidential SNAP records without citation to any specific RSA 91-A:5 subsection
  • Blanket attorney-client privilege invocations — applied categorically without document-specific analysis or privilege log, contrary to Mans v. Lebanon School Board, 112 N.H. 160 (1972)
  • Failure to address redaction alternatives — blanket denials without engaging specific alternatives proposed in both the court orders and SNAP records requests

St. Cyr's March 30 responses — line by line

Item 1 (Legal Unit review): "The Department's legal staff is tasked with the proper handling of all 91-A requests. The bureau operates in a very structured way to ensure all laws and rules are followed." Not responsive. The complaint documented specific instances of non-compliance. St. Cyr's response asserts general institutional compliance without addressing any specific documented instance. The documented contrast with Deputy Chief Counsel Martin's prompt and full RTK responses — noted in the RTK roadmap — was not addressed.

Item 2 (SNAP Category B records): "Although SNAP records are not public records, you are able to complete an authorization form for your own case records." Does not mention Category B. Does not mention audit logs. Does not mention inter-agency communications. Redirects to the authorization form process without engaging the Category A/Category B distinction Becker had explicitly made in the original request and every follow-up since September 2025.

Item 3 (RTK-6 redaction alternatives): "You have received our denial and the basis for the denial to each of your 91-A requests. There is no other information we can provide." Does not engage with the specific alternative — a single document retaining only a non-identifying checked checkbox. The complaint's documented failure to address redaction alternatives is repeated in the response to the complaint about that failure.

Item 4 (Legal Unit directive): "The Department's legal staff is required to follow 91-A for all requests it has received." Tautological. Does not address whether it did in these specific cases.

The March 31 exchange — matter "closed," then reopened

At 11:01 AM March 31, St. Cyr declared: "Unless you have additional requests that you have not yet sent, we consider this matter closed." Becker's 11:35 AM reply stripped the question to its binary core: "How do I obtain the system audit logs for October 11, 2024 showing which user account accessed Case #620663264 to modify household composition, and the system audit logs for November 4–6, 2024 showing which user account restored benefits?" He documented that this specific question had gone unanswered across six separate exchanges since September 25, 2025.

St. Cyr's 12:57 PM concession — the most significant line

St. Cyr's final response: "Thank you for clarifying your request. I have the team looking into whether there are logs and whether those logs are accessible. We are considering this a new right to know request and will be responding back to you in due course."

This single paragraph does three things that materially alter the record:

  • Concedes prior non-engagement: "Thank you for clarifying" acknowledges the prior six exchanges left the audit log question unanswered — directly supporting the complaint's core allegation
  • Acknowledges uncertainty about log existence: "Looking into whether there are logs" is remarkable for an agency with a SACWIS system and a SNAP system that both generate audit logs as standard functionality. The BEAR data team produced assessment statistics from Bridges in eight days. Whether logs exist for a specific date and case number should not require investigation.
  • Reclassifies a six-month-old request as new: The audit log request was first submitted September 25, 2025. It is not a new request. Reclassifying it resets the 30-business-day clock, routes it through the same Legal Unit whose handling is under complaint, and delays access without formally refusing it — precisely the pattern the complaint documented.
Procedural posture — RSA 91-A:7 petition
St. Cyr's partial engagement complicates but does not foreclose the RSA 91-A:7 petition posture. Item 3 was dismissed with finality. Items 1 and 4 were deflected. The Commissioner's delegation decision is documented. The audit log reclassification is a concession that Category B was not previously addressed — which strengthens, not weakens, the petition record. Commissioner Weaver is formally documented as aware of the complaint. Her delegation to the subject unit — without any indication of independent review — is itself a fact before the court on any RSA 91-A:7 petition.

Becker's final notation — September 25, 2025 origination

Becker's 1:10 PM response formally placed the origination date on record: "this request for the October 11, 2024 and November 4–6, 2024 system audit logs for Case #620663264 was first submitted on September 25, 2025 as Category B of my original SNAP records request, and has been raised in follow-up communications on October 28, 2025, December 29, 2025, February 19, 2026, and March 30, 2026." That documentation is now in the email thread — which will be exhibit material in any RSA 91-A:7 petition or federal proceeding. The "new RTK" clock, whenever it runs, cannot erase that six-month documented history of non-engagement.

System audit logs for Case #620663264 would reveal which user account made the October 11 modification — potentially implicating Surgento or Horne inter-agency communications with BFA. These logs are now being processed as a "new RTK" despite first being requested September 25, 2025.

St. Cyr — Intersecting Roles

Melissa A. St. Cyr, Esq., DHHS Chief Legal Officer. A single official whose documented roles now intersect across the AAU structural independence issue, the RTK records production decisions, the Hiring Waiver filing, the Commissioner complaint response, and the AAU hearing notice.
Structural convergence
St. Cyr is simultaneously: the official whose name appears on the nominally independent AAU's hearing notice; the CLO who received docket prioritization reports from Senior ALJ Cremmen; the point of contact on the AAU's Hiring Waiver predicting its own violations; the official who delegated and authored the Commissioner's response to a complaint about the Legal Unit; and the respondent-side CLO in the federal civil rights case. These roles do not belong to different people. They belong to one.

Role 1 — AAU hearing notice

The March 5, 2026 Notice of Hearing for Dockets 2026-0310 and 2026-0311 bears Melissa A. St. Cyr, Esq., Chief Legal Officer, on its institutional letterhead. The AAU is a nominally independent adjudicative body within DHHS. The respondent's top attorney appears on the face of the tribunal's official hearing communications. This document is now an exhibit in the related federal case (Case No. 1:24-cv-00430-JL-TSM).

The February 2, 2026 and December 17, 2025 emails document Senior ALJ Cremmen reporting AAU operational status and productivity to St. Cyr and receiving guidance on what is "important to the Department right now." The March 3, 2025 AAU Hiring Waiver Request — documenting that understaffing would produce federal regulatory violations — identified St. Cyr as the point of contact. The AAU predicted its own violations through a document filed by the respondent's CLO as the administrative point of contact for an independent adjudicative tribunal.

Role 4 — Commissioner complaint respondent

Becker's February 19, 2026 complaint to Commissioner Weaver requested an independent review of Legal Unit Coordinator Sittig's RSA 91-A compliance practices. Case No. 1:24-cv-00430-JL-TSM is pending in the District of New Hampshire. St. Cyr's name appears on the AAU hearing notice that is now an exhibit in that case. Her roles across the AAU proceedings, the RTK records access decisions, and the Commissioner complaint response are all potentially material to the constitutional claims in the federal case. Why this matters institutionally

The structural independence concern in the combined post-hearing motion is framed as a concern about the institution, not about any individual's good faith. That framing is correct and important for the legal argument. But the institutional concern is made concrete by the fact that one person occupies all five of these roles simultaneously. The convergence is not an argument about intent — it is a documented factual record of role intersection that RSA 541-A:36, the Due Process Clause, and federal civil rights law each address from different angles.
