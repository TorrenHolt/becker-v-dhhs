The Short Version

A Manchester, NH father repeatedly and explicitly told his child welfare caseworker she could not enter his home without a warrant. She acknowledged that right on tape — then entered his private stairwell 25 days later and interrogated his autistic teenage son across nine lines of inquiry while he was alone. A federal judge found those facts sufficient to state a constitutional claim. The case is now before the U.S. District Court on the caseworker's motion to dismiss, while three related administrative appeals and a state mandamus action run in parallel.

The Case Expands: Landmark Ruling, Administrative Appeals, and Mandamus

An Administrative Law Judge ruled on September 25, 2025 that DCYF's "Unfounded with Reasonable Concern" findings — a designation applied to ~10,500 families between 2020–2025 — are appealable, reversing DCYF's longstanding position that they were final and unreviewable. SURVIVING AFTER SCREENING
Count I
Fourth Amendment (McKenney only)

The Magistrate Judge found the allegations of the October 29 warrantless entry and interrogation of a minor child sufficient to state a claim. All other claims were dismissed – most on sovereign immunity grounds, some for failure to plausibly plead supervisory liability or conspiracy.

But the dismissed claims are not dead: they are part of the factual record in the AAU appeals, inform the request for injunctive relief, and demonstrate a pattern of conduct that defeats qualified immunity’s “clearly established” defense.

On October 29, 2024, DCYF Child Protective Services Worker Kimberly McKenney entered the private residential stairwell of Torrence Becker's third-floor apartment, climbed to his door, and conducted a seven-question investigative interview of his fifteen-year-old autistic son — without a warrant, without consent, and with actual personal knowledge that consent had been expressly refused.

Plaintiff alleges this was not an isolated incident, but part of a pattern: a safety plan containing disputed factual statements, coordination with code enforcement, and an unannounced contact with his minor son at the door of his apartment. When all three failed, DCYF tried again on November 7, 2024 — until the landlord turned McKenney away and told her she needed a warrant.

The United States Magistrate Judge found these allegations sufficient to state a Fourth Amendment claim. The case now turns on whether McKenney can claim qualified immunity for conduct she documented in her own records, using her own words.

The litigation has since expanded beyond the federal courthouse. A September 25, 2025 landmark ruling by AAU Administrative Law Judge Zachary Towle established that DCYF's Unfounded with Reasonable Concern findings are subject to administrative appeal — a designation DCYF had long maintained was unreviewable. Three pending administrative appeals and a mandamus petition filed in Merrimack County Superior Court on February 6, 2026 now run in parallel with the federal Fourth Amendment case, sharing the same factual core: a pattern of investigations conducted without procedural safeguards and an agency that resists accountability at every turn.

Landmark AAU Ruling: URC Findings Are Appealable

— Brian J. Goodchild, Esq., DCYF Supervising Staff Attorney · Proposed Finding of Fact #23 · October 3, 2025

The phrase "the children could return home" is not a neutral description of events. To advise a parent that children may return presupposes the authority to have determined they could not. DCYF's own supervising attorney, in a formal administrative filing, placed in writing that the agency exercised unilateral control over whether Becker's children were permitted to live in his home — from October 7 to October 14, 2024 — without any court order, petition, or hearing.

What "Could Return" Admits
  • → DCYF held authority to grant or deny home access
  • → Children were effectively removed from October 7–14
  • → Removal was at DCYF's discretion, not a court's
  • → No parent consented to this arrangement in writing

  • → Court order before any removal of custody
  • → Petition stating grounds under RSA 169-C
  • → Notice and opportunity to be heard
  • → Judicial finding of emergency circumstances

The Architecture of Privacy

Stairwell curtilage · Implied license revoked in writing · Knock-and-talk doctrine limits

The Defense argues McKenney entered a "common area." The building's own addressing structure disproves this. 162 Willow Street is not one unit among many sharing a hallway — it is an entire third floor, treated by the postal system, the landlord, and the building's architecture as a discrete, single-tenant dwelling equivalent to a townhouse.

The Mail Slot Is Dispositive

The United States Postal Service recognizes 162 Willow Street as a single, discrete address — not as "Apt. 3" or a subunit. The mail slot on Becker's porch door is active and functional. The slots on 158F and 160F are defunct. USPS addressing is the federal government's own determination of where a household begins and ends. It begins at the porch door marked "162."

The 160R Exterior Stairwell Establishes the Principle

The rear unit on the second floor — 160R — has its own private exterior stairwell. The building's architect and the landlord have already implemented the principle that rear or upper units may have single-tenant private access points. Becker's interior stairwell is the architectural equivalent for the third floor. The building's own design treats private stairwells as unit-specific, not communal.

Rheault Is Distinguishable

The Defense relies on United States v. Rheault, which addressed a common corridor serving multiple units in a traditional apartment building. In Rheault, the corridor provided through-access to multiple apartments. Here, the stairwell serves one unit only, bears one address only, requires one tenant's key only, and contains one tenant's personal property. Every Holleran factor distinguishes this space from a common hallway.

Fifteen Years of Exclusive Occupancy

Becker has occupied the stairwell exclusively for fifteen years — storing personal property, receiving mail, and treating it as the threshold of his home. Fifteen years of continuous exclusive use is the deepest subjective privacy expectation the law recognizes, and it is one that society is objectively prepared to acknowledge as reasonable.

The Dispositive Conclusion
162 Willow Street is functionally a townhouse. McKenney did not step into a common hallway — she opened a door marked with a single family's address, entered their exclusively used space, and climbed to their front door. The architecture, the postal addressing, the mail slot, and fifteen years of occupancy all point to the same answer: this was curtilage.

Controlling & Persuasive Authority

Florida v. Jardines, 569 U.S. 1 (2013)

Established that an implied license to approach a front door permits only approach, knock, and brief wait. The license does not include investigatory activity and may be revoked by the homeowner.

French v. Merrill, 15 F.4th 116 (1st Cir. 2021)

First Circuit: The implied license to approach a door "does not extend to investigatory activity." Officers conducting an investigation exceed the license even when physically permitted to approach.

United States v. Holleran, 2018 U.S. Dist. LEXIS 201359 (D. Mass.)

Six-factor test for determining whether an apartment area is protected curtilage or common area: well-traveled, locked access, number of tenants authorized, physical relationship to unit, right to control, open/enclosed. Every factor in this case supports curtilage designation.

Payton v. New York, 445 U.S. 573 (1980)

Warrantless entry into a home violates the Fourth Amendment absent exigent circumstances or consent. The home is the most constitutionally protected space. The rule applies with equal force to social workers as to police.

Calabretta v. Floyd, 189 F.3d 808 (9th Cir. 1999)

Social workers conducting home investigations without consent violate the Fourth Amendment. Privacy and parental liberty interests "apply with special force" where a state official questions children without consent or over parental objection.

City of Tahlequah v. Bond, 595 U.S. 9 (2021)

Qualified immunity "protects all but the plainly incompetent or those who knowingly violate the law." An official who personally acknowledges a constitutional right then violates it falls squarely within the "knowingly violates the law" category that immunity does not protect.

RSA 169-C:34, VII (New Hampshire)

State statute: When a parent refuses consent for home entry, DCYF "shall file a petition requesting the court for an ex parte order to enter the dwelling." The statute creates an explicit warrant-equivalent requirement — one DCYF bypassed entirely.

Schneckloth v. Bustamonte, 412 U.S. 218 (1973)

Consent to search must be voluntary — free from duress or coercion. Consent obtained under threat of adverse government action, including threatened child removal, does not meet the constitutional standard for valid consent.

The Appellate Preservation Issue
If a district court approves a magistrate judge's recommendation with only "after due consideration" and no substantive analysis, has it conducted the de novo review mandated by Rule 72(b)(3)? If de novo review was not conducted, the error is preserved for appeal.

Why This Case Matters

~10,500 NH families · No warrant compliance mechanism · Coercive tactics institutionalized · URC appealability now established

This case implicates fundamental questions that extend far beyond one family's experience with DCYF — questions about constitutional limits, agency accountability, administrative justice, and access to courts for those who cannot afford lawyers.

1

Individual Liberty Against State Power

The Fourth Amendment's protection is at its zenith in the home. The "chief evil" the Framers sought to prevent was arbitrary government intrusion into private dwellings. Can a state social worker enter your private residential entrance and question your child after you've explicitly refused consent — without a warrant, without exigent circumstances, and in direct violation of a state statute requiring court authorization?

The Question: If refusal of consent can be overcome simply by waiting a few weeks and returning unannounced, what constraint does the warrant requirement actually impose?

2

Inter-Agency Coordination as Constitutional Circumvention

If DCYF can coordinate with code enforcement to obtain evidence they cannot constitutionally obtain directly, every municipal agency becomes an extension of the investigative apparatus. The Fourth Amendment's warrant requirement becomes a speed bump, not a barrier. The timeline here is not circumstantial — it is documented, dated, and self-described in agency correspondence: consent refused Monday; code complaint filed three days later; inspection photographs requested for use in child welfare proceedings three weeks later.

The Question: If agencies can simply call a different department with different legal authority to obtain the same evidence, does the constitutional prohibition on warrantless searches have any practical effect?

3

Administrative Accountability and the Rule of Law

RSA 541-A:35 requires that final agency decisions include "findings of fact and conclusions of law, separately stated." This is not bureaucratic formalism — it is the minimum requirement for accountability and meaningful judicial review. Between 2020 and 2025, DCYF issued approximately 10,500 URC findings without findings of fact, without conclusions of law, without notice of appeal rights, and while actively maintaining such findings were unreviewable. The September 25 AAU ruling confirmed every one of those letters was legally deficient.

The Question: If state agencies can issue stigmatizing findings retained indefinitely — used as evidence of "patterns" in future proceedings — without explanation, without process, and without remedy, has administrative law become administrative fiat?

4

Access to Justice for Pro Se Litigants

The Supreme Court has mandated that pro se pleadings must be "construed liberally" and courts must "look to substance, not the label." Boag v. MacDougall, 454 U.S. 364 (1982). Most Americans cannot afford lawyers. This doctrine exists because the courthouse door must remain open to all. The procedural record here raises a specific question: when a pro se litigant files evidence that's never properly docketed, cites the correct procedural rule but is told it's wrong, receives contradictory orders, and files sixteen pages of specific objections that receive two sentences of response — has the system provided meaningful access?

The Question: When procedural gates become substantive barriers, has the courthouse door truly remained open?

The Convergence

This case sits at the intersection of:

  • → Constitutional law — Fourth Amendment protections against warrantless searches in the child welfare context
  • → Administrative law — Agency compliance with procedural requirements and the right to meaningful appeal
  • → Civil procedure — Pro se access to courts and the adequacy of de novo judicial review
  • → Qualified immunity — Whether government officials can claim immunity for violating clearly established rights they personally explained
  • → Systemic reform — Whether courts can order prospective relief to prevent ongoing violations across 10,500+ affected families
If the constitutional chains can be loosened through inter-agency coordination, indefinite administrative delays, and procedural barriers to judicial review, the constitutional structure erodes — not through dramatic change, but through a thousand small accommodations to government convenience.
