The Short Version

A Manchester, NH father repeatedly and explicitly told his child welfare caseworker she could not enter his home without a warrant. She acknowledged that right on tape — then entered his private stairwell 25 days later and interrogated his autistic teenage son across nine lines of inquiry while he was alone. A federal judge found those facts sufficient to state a constitutional claim. The case is now before the U.S. District Court on the caseworker's motion to dismiss, while three related administrative appeals and a state mandamus action run in parallel.

SURVIVING AFTER SCREENING
Count I
Fourth Amendment (McKenney only)

The Magistrate Judge found the allegations of the October 29 warrantless entry and interrogation of a minor child sufficient to state a claim. All other claims were dismissed – most on sovereign immunity grounds, some for failure to plausibly plead supervisory liability or conspiracy.

But the dismissed claims are not dead: they are part of the factual record in the AAU appeals, inform the request for injunctive relief, and demonstrate a pattern of conduct that defeats qualified immunity’s “clearly established” defense.

On October 29, 2024, DCYF Child Protective Services Worker Kimberly McKenney entered the private residential stairwell of Torrence Becker's third-floor apartment, climbed to his door, and conducted a seven-question investigative interview of his fifteen-year-old autistic son — without a warrant, without consent, and with actual personal knowledge that consent had been expressly refused.

Plaintiff alleges this was not an isolated incident, but part of a pattern: a safety plan containing disputed factual statements, coordination with code enforcement, and an unannounced contact with his minor son at the door of his apartment. When all three failed, DCYF tried again on November 7, 2024 — until the landlord turned McKenney away and told her she needed a warrant.

The United States Magistrate Judge found these allegations sufficient to state a Fourth Amendment claim. The case now turns on whether McKenney can claim qualified immunity for conduct she documented in her own records, using her own words.

The litigation has since expanded beyond the federal courthouse. A September 25, 2025 landmark ruling by AAU Administrative Law Judge Zachary Towle established that DCYF's Unfounded with Reasonable Concern findings are subject to administrative appeal — a designation DCYF had long maintained was unreviewable. Three pending administrative appeals and a mandamus petition filed in Merrimack County Superior Court on February 6, 2026 now run in parallel with the federal Fourth Amendment case, sharing the same factual core: a pattern of investigations conducted without procedural safeguards and an agency that resists accountability at every turn.

The Mail Slot Is Dispositive

The United States Postal Service recognizes 162 Willow Street as a single, discrete address — not as "Apt. 3" or a subunit. The mail slot on Becker's porch door is active and functional. The slots on 158F and 160F are defunct. USPS addressing is the federal government's own determination of where a household begins and ends. It begins at the porch door marked "162."

The 160R Exterior Stairwell Establishes the Principle

The rear unit on the second floor — 160R — has its own private exterior stairwell. The building's architect and the landlord have already implemented the principle that rear or upper units may have single-tenant private access points. Becker's interior stairwell is the architectural equivalent for the third floor. The building's own design treats private stairwells as unit-specific, not communal.

Rheault Is Distinguishable

The Defense relies on United States v. Rheault, which addressed a common corridor serving multiple units in a traditional apartment building. In Rheault, the corridor provided through-access to multiple apartments. Here, the stairwell serves one unit only, bears one address only, requires one tenant's key only, and contains one tenant's personal property. Every Holleran factor distinguishes this space from a common hallway.

Fifteen Years of Exclusive Occupancy

Becker has occupied the stairwell exclusively for fifteen years — storing personal property, receiving mail, and treating it as the threshold of his home. Fifteen years of continuous exclusive use is the deepest subjective privacy expectation the law recognizes, and it is one that society is objectively prepared to acknowledge as reasonable.

The Dispositive Conclusion
162 Willow Street is functionally a townhouse. McKenney did not step into a common hallway — she opened a door marked with a single family's address, entered their exclusively used space, and climbed to their front door. The architecture, the postal addressing, the mail slot, and fifteen years of occupancy all point to the same answer: this was curtilage.

Appeal Filed — NH Supreme Court No. 2025-0409 · Brief Filed August 1, 2025 Becker appealed the dismissal pro se, framing a question of first impression: whether RSA 91-A provides an enforcement mechanism when agencies withhold records from parents who already have statutory access rights under RSA 170-G:8-a — or whether agencies enjoy complete immunity from transparency enforcement simply by invoking that statute. The brief presents five independent grounds for reversal.
Argument B — Waiver & Judicial Estoppel strength: factually strong / legally uncertain
For eight months — October 2024 through June 2025 — DCYF processed this request under RSA 91-A procedures without once citing RSA 170-G:8-a. Administrator Heiple's January 30, 2025 acknowledgment ("The District Office is determining how to furnish the audio recordings") mirrors the exact language of RSA 91-A:4. Most significantly, when records were finally produced on the courthouse steps, they were handed over without redaction, without mandatory confidentiality disclaimers, and on a generic receipt form — the complete absence of the confidentiality protocols RSA 170-G actually requires. The argument: you cannot claim categorical confidentiality protection in court while simultaneously failing to implement confidentiality procedures in practice. Cite: State Employees' Ass'n v. Belknap County, 122 N.H. 614 (1982). The factual predicate is unusually strong; the legal question of whether government agencies can waive a statutory confidentiality scheme through conduct is less settled.
Argument C — Harmonious Construction strength: structurally clean — stronger than the brief states
The brief proposes a division of labor between the two statutes that eliminates any conflict: RSA 170-G:8-a answers the substantive question of who has access rights (parents, children, guardians — not the general public); RSA 91-A answers the procedural question of how those rights are enforced when an agency fails to comply. A stranger invoking RSA 91-A alone would lack standing — they have no access rights under RSA 170-G to enforce. Only those with pre-existing 170-G entitlements can invoke 91-A as an enforcement mechanism.
This argument is stronger than the brief's current framing reflects. RSA 91-A is not displacing 170-G:8-a — it is supplying the enforcement infrastructure that 170-G structurally lacks and never provided. RSA 170-G:8-aa, the statute's own internal review mechanism, contains no bad faith finding, no fee-shifting, no civil penalty, no contempt mechanism, and no expedited hearing right. It delivers an administrative determination with nothing behind it. RSA 91-A provides all of those remedies. The two statutes are not in tension — they are incomplete without each other. The legislature created an access right in 170-G and a set of enforcement tools in 91-A; harmonious construction treats them as the integrated scheme they functionally must be.
Argument D — Lambert Narrow Exemption Rule strength: standard — depends on court's framing
Under Lambert v. Belknap County Convention, 157 N.H. 375 (2008), the NH Supreme Court requires that "provisions favoring disclosure" be construed broadly and exemptions construed narrowly. The trial court did the opposite: it read RSA 170-G:8-a as a categorical displacement of RSA 91-A's entire enforcement framework — an expansive reading of an exemption. The brief also invokes Goode v. N.H. Legislative Budget Assistant, 148 N.H. 551 (2002), which requires "sufficient explicit language prohibiting disclosure" before a statute can be read to exempt records from RSA 91-A. RSA 170-G:8-a contains no such language — it does not mention RSA 91-A, does not say its access provisions are exclusive, and does not state that no other enforcement mechanisms apply.
Argument E — The Structural Enforcement Gap strength: the strongest argument — transcends this case
The brief frames the enforcement gap as a stonewall problem: if DCYF never issues a formal denial, RSA 170-G:8-aa cannot be triggered, and under the trial court's ruling RSA 91-A is unavailable, leaving the parent with no remedy. That is accurate — but it understates the problem.
The gap is not limited to the stonewall scenario. RSA 170-G:8-aa has no teeth regardless of whether a formal denial is issued. Even when DCYF does everything procedurally correct — formally denies access, triggering the 170-G:8-aa review pathway — that pathway delivers an administrative determination with no enforcement mechanism behind it. There is no bad faith finding available, no fee-shifting, no civil penalty, no contempt, no expedited hearing right. A parent who receives a formal denial and follows the 170-G:8-aa process to its conclusion is left with a piece of paper. The enforcement gap is therefore structural and complete: it exists whether the agency stonewalls (Scenario 1: no formal denial, 170-G:8-aa never triggered) or whether it formally denies (Scenario 2: 170-G:8-aa triggered but remedyless). In both scenarios, without RSA 91-A, there is no enforcement. The access right in RSA 170-G is a right without a remedy — which, functionally, is not a right at all.
This argument does not depend on the specific facts of this case. It is a structural critique of what the trial court's ruling produces and will recur wherever DCYF or any agency faces a records demand under this statutory framework.
Argument F — Due Process / Denied Briefing Time strength: weakest of the five
When DCYF raised RSA 170-G:8-a for the first time at the June 4 hearing, Becker immediately requested time to research and brief the issue. The court denied the request and ruled on a question of first impression without briefing from either party. The brief cites Murray v. N.H. Div. of State Police, 154 N.H. 579 (2006) and Mathews v. Eldridge, 424 U.S. 319 (1976). This argument is factually accurate but difficult to win in isolation — appellate courts are reluctant to reverse on procedural grounds where the legal issue might have gone the same way with full briefing. Its primary value here is in combination with the other arguments: the denial of briefing is more significant when the issue ruled on was genuinely novel.
Appeal Status — Under Consideration The appeal is pending but may be withdrawn. The case facts present a complicated vehicle: records were produced before the hearing, mooting immediate relief, and the due process argument alone is unlikely to carry the day. The enforcement gap argument (Argument E) and the harmonious construction argument (Argument C) are the most legally significant — but they may find a stronger vehicle in one of the other RTK threads, where the structural problem can be presented on a cleaner factual record without the complication of last-minute production.

The Appellate Preservation Issue

Why This Case Matters

~19,400 NH families · No warrant compliance mechanism · Coercive tactics institutionalized · URC appealability established · Rule 72(b)(3) institutional question preserved

This case implicates fundamental questions that extend far beyond one family's experience with DCYF — questions about constitutional limits, agency accountability, administrative justice, and access to courts for those who cannot afford lawyers.

1

Individual Liberty Against State Power

The Fourth Amendment's protection is at its zenith in the home. The "chief evil" the Framers sought to prevent was arbitrary government intrusion into private dwellings. Can a state social worker enter your private residential entrance and question your child after you've explicitly refused consent — without a warrant, without exigent circumstances, and in direct violation of a state statute requiring court authorization?

The Question: If refusal of consent can be overcome simply by waiting a few weeks and returning unannounced, what constraint does the warrant requirement actually impose?

2

Inter-Agency Coordination as Constitutional Circumvention

If DCYF can coordinate with code enforcement to obtain evidence they cannot constitutionally obtain directly, every municipal agency becomes an extension of the investigative apparatus. The Fourth Amendment's warrant requirement becomes a speed bump, not a barrier. The timeline here is not circumstantial — it is documented, dated, and self-described in agency correspondence: consent refused Monday; code complaint filed three days later; inspection photographs requested for use in child welfare proceedings three weeks later.

The Question: If agencies can simply call a different department with different legal authority to obtain the same evidence, does the constitutional prohibition on warrantless searches have any practical effect?

3

Administrative Accountability and the Rule of Law

RSA 541-A:35 requires that final agency decisions include "findings of fact and conclusions of law, separately stated." This is not bureaucratic formalism — it is the minimum requirement for accountability and meaningful judicial review. Between 2017 and 2026, DCYF issued approximately 19,400 URC findings without findings of fact, without conclusions of law, without notice of appeal rights, and while actively maintaining such findings were unreviewable. The December 4, 2017 workgroup minutes (Exhibit I-2) prove the deficiency was identified before the first letter was ever sent. The September 25 AAU ruling confirmed every one of those letters was legally deficient.

The Question: If state agencies can issue stigmatizing findings retained indefinitely — used as evidence of "patterns" in future proceedings — without explanation, without process, and without remedy, has administrative law become administrative fiat?

4

Access to Justice for Pro Se Litigants

The Supreme Court has mandated that pro se pleadings must be "construed liberally" and courts must "look to substance, not the label." Boag v. MacDougall, 454 U.S. 364 (1982). Most Americans cannot afford lawyers. This doctrine exists because the courthouse door must remain open to all. The procedural record here raises a specific question: when a pro se litigant files evidence that's never properly docketed, cites the correct procedural rule but is told it's wrong, receives contradictory orders, and files sixteen pages of specific objections that receive two sentences of response — has the system provided meaningful access?

The Question: When procedural gates become substantive barriers, has the courthouse door truly remained open?

5

The Rule 72(b)(3) Question — An Institutional Issue

This case has produced a four-order record showing that the District of New Hampshire responds to pro se civil rights objections — regardless of length, specificity, or constitutional complexity — with an identical one-sentence formula. Two different Article III judges. Two different cases. The same sentence, word for word, every time. The objections ranged from 16 to 28 pages and raised specific constitutional arguments, precise legal errors, and admissible post-R&R evidence. None of it changed the response.

Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 72(b)(3) uses the word "must": a district judge must determine de novo any part of the magistrate judge's disposition that has been properly objected to. United States v. Raddatz, 447 U.S. 667 (1980), confirmed this requires meaningful review. A formula that never names an argument, never rejects or modifies a finding, and never engages with the specific grounds for objection is not de novo review. It is a default approval.

The stakes here extend beyond this case. Every pro se civil rights litigant in New Hampshire who files Rule 72(b) objections is entitled to the review Raddatz requires. The district consistently invokes Santos-Santos v. Torres-Centeno, 842 F.3d 163 (1st Cir. 2016), against litigants who fail to object — failure to object waives appellate review. That doctrine cannot operate only in one direction. It forecloses the litigant who does not object. It must also mean something to the litigant who does.

The Question: If the district court's response to a 28-page objection raising Bumper, three Ex parte Young bases, and agency admissions from the government's own files is the same sentence it uses for a routine filing, does the preservation mechanism the district cites against pro se litigants operate symmetrically — or only in the government's favor?

The Convergence

This case sits at the intersection of:

  • → Constitutional law — Fourth Amendment protections against warrantless searches in the child welfare context
  • → Administrative law — Agency compliance with procedural requirements and the right to meaningful appeal
  • → Civil procedure — Pro se access to courts and the adequacy of de novo judicial review
  • → Qualified immunity — Whether government officials can claim immunity for violating clearly established rights they personally explained
  • → Systemic reform — Whether courts can order prospective relief to prevent ongoing violations across 19,400+ affected families
  • → Federal judicial procedure — Whether the District of New Hampshire applies Rule 72(b)(3) de novo review to pro se civil rights objections, or whether the four-order formula pattern constitutes an institutional practice that the First Circuit must examine
If the constitutional chains can be loosened through inter-agency coordination, indefinite administrative delays, and procedural barriers to judicial review, the constitutional structure erodes — not through dramatic change, but through a thousand small accommodations to government convenience.
